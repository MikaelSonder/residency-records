Executive Summary

Immigration law in the United States is the professional legal and administrative function through which foreign nationals obtain lawful entry, temporary stay, work authorization, permanent residence or related immigration status in the country for a defined purpose.

Operationally, U.S. immigration matters often begin with route selection, including family-based immigration, employment-based temporary worker classifications, immigrant visa pathways, adjustment of status and longer-term lawful permanent residence through the Green Card system.

The U.S. system is category-driven and highly procedural. In practice, the correct route depends on family relationship, employer sponsorship, current status, admissibility, place of filing and whether the person seeks temporary work authorization or permanent residence.

Cross-border relevance is substantial because immigration planning in the United States may interact with consular processing, multinational employment structures, tax residence, family mobility and later naturalization or long-term residence strategy.

Process Flow

The process flow explains how immigration law work usually progresses from route assessment to application outcome and later status maintenance. It matters because immigration law is an operating sequence, not a single form submission.

1. Status MappingIdentify nationality, current location, intended purpose of stay, family structure, employer position and whether the objective is temporary stay or permanent residence.
2. Route SelectionDetermine whether the correct pathway is family-based immigration, temporary worker classification, employment-based immigrant route, work authorization category or another lawful basis.
3. Eligibility ReviewAssess whether the factual background, sponsorship structure and legal criteria support the chosen route.
4. Documentary PreparationGather identity records, civil-status documents, employer materials, immigration history, financial evidence and route-specific supporting records.
5. Petition or Application FilingSubmit the relevant USCIS form, petition or related filing, and coordinate consular or in-country processing as applicable.
6. Agency Review and Follow-UpRespond to requests for evidence, interview requirements, biometrics, status questions or consular steps where applicable.
7. Status Maintenance or TransitionMaintain lawful status, renew where necessary and prepare transition toward permanent residence, work continuity or other long-term immigration objectives.
Decision Tree

The decision tree simplifies threshold questions that commonly determine the correct immigration law route. It is presented as a logical workflow so the reader can follow the operational sequence rather than treat each route as an isolated label.

  1. Identify who the applicant is, where the person is located and why entry or residence in the United States is sought.
  2. Confirm whether the objective is temporary stay, temporary work authorization or lawful permanent residence.
  3. Determine whether the case is based on family relationship, employer sponsorship, humanitarian protection or another statutory category.
  4. Check whether the process requires employer petitioning, family petitioning, consular processing or an in-country application route.
  5. Assess whether work authorization exists automatically, requires a specific status or requires an Employment Authorization Document.
  6. Prepare a coherent file that matches the route selected and maintain continuity after filing and after decision.
